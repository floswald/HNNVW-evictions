Discussion Notes: Humphries, Nelson, Nguyen, van Dijk & Waldinger

Nonpayment and Eviction in the Rental Housing Market

Author

Florian Oswald

Published

April 27, 2026

What the Paper Does

The paper has three parts. First, novel data: lease-level ledger records from ~6,000 low-income tenancies in several Midwestern US cities, 2015–2019. This is the first dataset linking month-by-month rent payment histories to landlord eviction filing decisions. The sample is professionally managed units in low-income neighborhoods (median rent ~$950, median household income ~$2,800/month, eviction filing rate 5.6% vs 3.4% county-wide).

Key descriptive facts worth flagging: half of tenants default at some point; across all tenants, 91% of rent due is collected (86% for the unsubsidized no-voucher subsample used in estimation) — a loss rate exceeding US credit cards, mortgages, and high-yield bonds. Yet landlords typically wait 2–3 months before filing. Among first-time defaulters, 39% fully repay their balance. This tolerance is the paper’s central puzzle.

Second, a structural model: a Bayesian landlord observes monthly payment outcomes \(y_t \in \{0,1,2\}\) (no pay / current rent / current + repayment) and updates beliefs \(\pi_t\) over a tenant’s latent type \(\omega_t\) governed by a Markov process. Filing costs \(C_e \approx \$2,000\) (2–3 months rent). By the time the median landlord files, the posterior puts 75% probability on the lowest type.

Third, counterfactuals: a $250 eviction tax, a procedural delay, and short-term rental assistance (SRA) — each calibrated to cut eviction rates by 5%. The three policies differ sharply in cost and in which evictions they prevent.

Comment 1: What is a “Type”?

The Markov process \(\omega_t \sim F(\cdot|\omega_{t-1})\) is doing double duty. A tenant’s “type” in the model is meant to capture their underlying propensity to pay — think of it as their financial discipline, their personal threshold of pain before defaulting, their innate honesty about debt obligations. This is a genuine type that varies across people and is persistent.

But the same process also captures transitory shocks: unemployment spells, medical bills, family emergencies. A fundamentally reliable tenant who loses her job for three months looks identical in the data to a medium type. She generates the same payment history, produces the same posterior \(\pi_t\), and the landlord (and econometrician) cannot distinguish her from a genuinely worse tenant.

This conflation matters because the policy implications are opposite:

  • If nonpayment is primarily type-driven (persistent bad actors), SRA is wasted on people who will default again; delays just keep bad tenants in units longer; a tax prevents few evictions because landlords are already right to evict.
  • If nonpayment is primarily shock-driven (good people with bad luck), SRA timed to the shock is nearly first-best; delays give tenants time to recover; a tax might prevent the “right” evictions.

The paper’s Table 6 is implicitly about this: under less persistent types (closer to the shock interpretation), the same $250 tax cuts evictions 6× more. The estimated high persistence (\(L \to L = 96.4\%\)) is what drives the limited policy scope — but it conflates genuine persistence of type with the absence of shock modelling.

There is also a third source of variation in \(\omega_t\) that the model conflates with the other two: peer effects. Campaniello & Macaluso (2026) study rent delinquency in Turin public housing using quasi-random assignment via lottery and score-based ranking. Their main result: a 10pp increase in neighbor delinquency raises own delinquency by 3pp (8% of the 37% baseline). The mechanism runs through social norms — language-proximate neighbors have a 7× larger effect, consistent with delinquency norms spreading through social networks rather than pure financial mimicry. In the HNNDW setting, this means drift in \(\omega_t\) may reflect neither a transitory income shock nor innate character, but a social-norm update: living among non-payers normalizes non-payment. The Markov process cannot distinguish this from genuine type change. This strengthens the case for thinking carefully about what “type persistence” means — a high \(L \to L\) transition rate could partly reflect persistent peer exposure, not irreversible personal characteristics.

There is also no discussion of strategic default. The tenant’s payment decision is purely mechanical: draw type, pay with probability \(\theta(\omega_t)\). No optimization. The paper mentions (Section 4.4) that asymmetric information and limited commitment prevent cooperative solutions, and Section 6.3 does a reduced-form moral hazard robustness check (cutting the middle type’s payment rate by half the post-filing drop). But this is not a model of tenant behavior.

An interesting data point: post-filing, the proportional reduction in payment probability is \(\phi_1 = 0.86\), meaning an evicted Type L tenant (baseline payment 4.2%) still pays 3.6% of the time. Is any of that strategic — paying just enough to slow the removal process? If so, the payment history contains less information about the tenant’s underlying type than the model assumes.

Comment 2: The \(\epsilon\) Payoff Shock

Equation (8) writes the eviction choice probability as a standard logit: \[P(e=1 \mid \pi, b) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(\bar{v}^{e=0} - \bar{v}^{e=1})}\] with no scale parameter in the denominator. This looks like the standard Rust (1987) normalization where \(\sigma_\epsilon \equiv 1\).

But Table 4 reports “S.D. epsilon = $581 (34)” as an estimated parameter with a standard error. So \(\sigma_\epsilon\) is estimated — it is just absorbed silently into the notation of equation (8), presumably because the choice-specific value functions \(\bar{v}\) are expressed in \(\sigma_\epsilon\)-normalized units.

Why can \(\sigma_\epsilon\) be identified here when Rust must normalize? In Rust’s bus engine model, flow utility has no natural scale. The only dollar-denominated object is the replacement cost parameter, but it appears inside the utility function with no external anchor. So only \(C_e / \sigma_\epsilon\) is identified and the normalization \(\sigma_\epsilon = 1\) is innocuous.

Here, the flow payoff \(R \cdot y_t - e_t C_e - c\) is in observed dollars: rent \(R\) is in the data and varies across units. This provides an external dollar anchor. \(\sigma_\epsilon\) is identified from the cross-state variation in eviction probabilities: as \(\Delta\bar{v}(\pi, b)\) varies across belief-balance states, the steepness of \(P(e=1)\) as a function of \(\Delta\bar{v}\) identifies \(1/\sigma_\epsilon\), while the level pins down \(C_e / \sigma_\epsilon\) separately.

The concern: \(\Delta\bar{v}(\pi, b)\) is not observed — it is computed by solving the dynamic program at candidate parameter values. So identification of \(\sigma_\epsilon\) runs through the nonlinear structural fixed-point. If the model is misspecified — wrong number of types, wrong Markov structure, truncated balance state space (\(b\) capped at 4 months) — the misfit will load onto \(\sigma_\epsilon\). The estimated \(\hat\sigma_\epsilon = \$581\) is 29% of \(\hat C_e = \$1,977\): large enough that the eviction probability never gets close to 0 or 1 even in the most extreme belief states. This is a lot of unexplained variation in the eviction decision.

The fact that the paper’s own presentation omits \(\sigma_\epsilon\) from its key equation is a mild but real concern: it suggests the parameter’s role is not fully appreciated. At minimum, the paper should report what share of the residual variation in eviction decisions (conditional on \(\pi\) and \(b\)) \(\sigma_\epsilon\) accounts for.

Discussion Point 1: Tax Beats Delay — The Mechanism

The key policy finding is that a $250 eviction tax dominates a procedural delay of equivalent size (in terms of eviction reduction) on almost every dimension: lower cost to landlords ($6.28 vs $8.20/unit-month), lower net social cost ($0.91 vs $8.20/unit-month), and — most importantly — it saves the right evictions.

The mechanism is subtle. A tax raises the fixed cost of filing, making the filing decision more sensitive at the margin. The tenants on the margin are those the landlord is uncertain about — i.e., those with intermediate posteriors, which skews toward medium and high types who are more likely to recover. 21.6% of tenants whose evictions are delayed or prevented by the tax would resume paying, vs. 14.7% of all evictees at baseline.

A delay works differently. It raises the cost of an eviction by extending the post-filing period during which the landlord is stuck with a non-paying tenant who is hard to remove. This cost is largest precisely for the lowest-type tenants — they pay almost nothing post-filing and stay the longest. So a delay deters filing most for Type L evictions, which are the ones that should happen (only 0.1% of Type L evictions delayed by delay result in recovery). The result: delay saves mostly the tenants least likely to recover, at the highest cost to landlords.

The table from Table 7 makes this stark:

Type H saved (%) Type M saved (%) Type L saved (%) Net cost ($/unit-mo)
Tax 30 19 10 0.91
Delay 6 9 12 8.20

The obvious challenge to raise: is delay being modeled too narrowly? The paper captures right-to-counsel only through \(\delta_e\) (the post-filing tenant exit rate). But right-to-counsel also: reduces the probability of a possession judgment, generates negotiated repayment agreements, and may deter filing altogether in some cases. The Collinson et al. (2024a) NYC evidence finds that legal aid reduced judgment rates and affected case outcomes beyond just delay. If the authors modeled these additional channels, the comparison might look more favorable to delay.

Discussion Point 2: The 5% Result and Type Persistence

The headline finding is that all three policies reduce evictions by only 5% when calibrated to a $250 tax. This is the paper’s most important quantitative claim for policy, and it rests entirely on two estimated parameters:

  1. High eviction cost (\(C_e = \$2,000\)): landlords find eviction expensive, so they wait. Waiting generates information, so by filing time they are quite sure the tenant is bad. Few filings are marginal.

  2. High persistence of the low type (\(L \to L = 96.4\%\)): once a tenant is identified as Type L, they almost certainly stay Type L. The option value of waiting for them to recover is low, so when landlords do file, they file on tenants who are almost certainly going to keep not paying.

Table 6 is the key sensitivity check: under a less persistent type process (convex combination of estimated \(M\) and uniform), the same $250 tax cuts evictions by 33% rather than 5% — a 6× difference.

The identification question: How is persistence estimated? From the payment histories of non-evicted tenants, under the assumption that conditional on the full payment history \(h_t\), the landlord’s eviction decision is uncorrelated with future payments. This is the conditional independence / selection-on-observables assumption standard in DDC models (Magnac and Thesmar, 2002). It implies that the landlord’s information set is exactly what the econometrician observes: the payment history.

This assumption is directly tested by the full-information robustness: a model where the landlord perfectly observes the tenant’s current type. The full-information model fits the data comparably (log-likelihood –10,361 vs –10,361 for the learning model — identical to reported precision), but requires \(C_e = \$3,550\) rather than $1,977 to rationalize the same slow eviction pace. The reason: if the landlord knows the tenant is Type L but waits several months to file, the model needs a very high filing cost to make that rational.

These two models — learning and full-information — bracket the truth. The learning model gives the lower bound on landlord information and an upper bound on the fraction of evictees who would recover. The full-information model gives the opposite bound. The 5% policy scope result holds qualitatively in both, but the full-information model estimates fewer recoverable evictions (only 2.3% of evictees would resume paying, vs 14.7% in the learning model). So the targeting advantage of the tax over delay is diminished under full information.

The building-sales IV strategy for testing the information assumption is acknowledged as underpowered. An interesting alternative might be to use variation in landlord experience: new landlords vs. experienced ones should update their beliefs differently if the learning mechanism is real. Or variation in court processing times, which affects the cost structure exogenously.

Q&A Clarifications

Q: How does the counterfactual actually work — isn’t re-solving the value function circular?

No. There are two separate loops. During estimation, the Bellman equations are solved repeatedly at each candidate parameter vector \(\theta\) to compute choice probabilities for the likelihood. Once \(\hat\theta\) is pinned down, estimation is done. The counterfactual takes \(\hat\theta\) as fixed, changes one policy parameter (e.g. \(C_e \to C_e + 250\)), and re-solves the Bellman equations once under the new parameter. No data are used — it is pure model simulation. Re-solving is necessary because the optimal eviction threshold shifts non-linearly with \(C_e\) due to option value; you cannot just subtract $250 from a regression coefficient.

Q: Where does the 91% rent collection figure come from — why not 86%?

Table 1 (main paper), column (a) “All tenants”: 90.9%. The 86.2% figure is column (d), the no-voucher estimation subsample only. The correct headline number for the full sample is 91%.

Q: Where does the 6× figure come from?

Table 6 of the paper (“Counterfactual Results with Less Persistent Tenant Types”). Under the estimated parameters, a $250 tax reduces the eviction rate from 2.27% to 2.15% (−5.3%). Under less persistent types (2:1 convex combination of estimated \(M\) and uniform), the same tax reduces the rate from 2.27% to 1.52% (−33%). The ratio is 33/5.3 ≈ 6×. The paper’s own text on p. 35 states this as “six times as effective.”

Q: Where does the $3,550 figure come from?

Table 4, column (3): the 3-type full-information model estimates \(\hat C_e = \$3,550\) (vs $1,977 in the baseline learning model). To rationalize why a landlord who knows the tenant is Type L still waits months to file, the model needs much higher filing costs. The two models bracket the truth about the landlord’s information set.

Q: Why does delay deter Type L evictions most — shouldn’t the landlord always file on Type L?

Yes — filing on Type L is still better than not filing (the alternative is the tenant staying ~27 months at near-zero rent, since \(\delta_d = 0.037\)). But delay shifts the timing threshold: “file now” becomes less attractive because the post-filing period is extended and Type L pays almost nothing during it. So landlords wait a few more months before pulling the trigger. The evictions mostly still happen — they are deferred, not prevented. Table 7 confirms: under Delay, Type L “Never Evicted” = 1.74%, barely different from Tax (1.52%). The cost difference ($8.20 vs $0.91/unit-month) comes from the landlord being stuck with a near-zero-paying tenant for longer both pre- and post-filing.

Q: What exactly is the “5% result”?

A reduction in the eviction rate, not the share of evictions. Baseline: 2.27% per unit-month. All three policies (calibrated to a $250 tax): 2.15%. That is a 5.3% reduction in the rate, or about 0.12 percentage points in absolute terms. The rate barely moves because by filing time, landlords are already highly confident the tenant is Type L, so raising the cost does not change the filing decision at the margin for most evictions.

Summary

Four things to say at the end:

  1. The data contribution is the paper’s most durable result. The descriptive facts — 39% recovery rate, 2–3 month tolerance window, non-monotone eviction hazard — will be cited for years regardless of the model.

  2. The Tax-beats-Delay result is the most policy-relevant finding. It directly challenges the prevailing advocacy for right-to-counsel as the primary eviction-prevention tool. But the delay parametrization is narrow.

  3. The 5% scope result depends critically on type persistence, which is identified under a conditional independence assumption that cannot be directly tested with the available instruments. The paper should be clearer about this fragility.

  4. The 15% recoverable evictions figure is actionable. Targeted SRA — if tenants can be identified at the time of crisis — is the efficient intervention. This is the natural next paper.
